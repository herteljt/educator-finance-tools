Scholarships are tax-free money — which means they're worth more than the same amount earned at a job. This tool helps you figure out your effective hourly rate from applying, and how that compares to just working more hours.

STEP 1

Your Scholarship Application

$
The dollar value of the award if you win.
hrs
Total hours writing essays, gathering materials, and submitting.
%
Rough % of applications you expect to win. 20% is a reasonable starting estimate.
STEP 2

Your Job (for comparison)

$/hr
What you earn (or would earn) per hour at a job.
%
The % of your next earned dollar that goes to taxes. Most college students: 10–22%. Unsure? Use 12%.
Why does tax rate matter? Wages are taxed; scholarship money generally isn't (for tuition and fees). A $1,000 scholarship is worth the same as earning roughly $1,136 at a job if your tax rate is 12% — because you'd owe about $136 in taxes on the wages but nothing on the scholarship.

How to read this: Your effective hourly rate from applying accounts for the fact that you won't win every scholarship — it spreads the expected value of winning across all the time you spend applying (including the applications you don't win). If that rate beats your after-tax hourly wage, applying for scholarships is a better use of your time, financially speaking.
